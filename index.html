Research Overview

An Analysis of Dimensionality Expansion Performed on Quantum and Classical Support Vector Machines
WORK IN PROGRESS

Ever since the discovery of the Support Vector Machine (SVM) algorithm, SVMs have been among one of the best supervised machine learning algorithms to classify linear and non-linear data. Unfortunately, SVMs possess kernel limitations when introduced to higher dimensional data and while working with problems containing large features.

With the introduction of quantum computation and quantum machine learning, researchers have successfully implemented a Quantum Support Vector Machine (QSVM) onto a quantum computer. Doing so has allowed classical data to be transferred into quantum states, taking advantage of the large dimensionality of quantum Hilbert space.

To study the effectiveness and accuracy of dimensionality expansion in QSVMs as opposed to SVMs, we processed dimensionally reduced data into SVMs and QSVMs. Using evaluation metrics and techniques the processed datasets are compared and analyzed.

The Deutsch-Jozsa Algorithm

Proposed by David Deutsch and Richard Jozsa in 1992, the Deutsch-Jozsa Algorithm was one of the first quantum algorithms that showed that quantum computation could demonstrate an exponential speed-up over a deterministic classical algorithm. The Deutsch-Jozsa Algorithm was the inspiration for Simon's and Shor's Algorithm.

Project Overview

Quantum PvP

Quantum PvP is a game that starts with a quantum circuit with 3 qubits in superposition. 2 players take turns applying quantum gates onto the circuit, trying to manipulate the qubits, such that they collapse in a specific state. Players have 3 turns each and may add [X], [Y], [Z], [Rx], [Ry], [Rz], [CNOT], or the [SWAP] gate onto the circuit.

Project Overview

Quantum Machine Learning with QAOA

Last week, I was given an amazing opportunity to participate in a quantum computing hackathon. With amazing mentors from QOSF (The Quantum Open Source Foundation), my team used Rigetti's quantum computer and created a quantum machine learning program that clustered flowers based on their attributes. We then compared our program to classical machine learning and was amazed by the results.


Using the k-means clustering algorithm , we were able to achieve 100% accuracy in clustering 8 data samples in a time of 35.7 ms on a local CPU. With Rigetti's Quantum Optimization Approximation Algorithm (QAOA) and a weighted MaxCut clustering technique, we were able to achieve 100% accuracy in clustering 8 data samples in a time of 92,000 ms on Rigetti's Quantum Virtual Machine.
